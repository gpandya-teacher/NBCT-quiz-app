## Acute kidney injury (3 items)

- **Acute tubular necrosis** — Acute tubular necrosis is the most likely cause of AKI following significant intraoperative hypotension or blood loss. The proximal tubule is highly susceptible to ischemia due to its high ATP demand. Key lab findings in ATN include elevated urine sodium (>40 mEq/L), FENa >1%, and BUN/Cr ratio <20 — in contrast to prerenal azotemia, which shows low urine sodium and high BUN/Cr. ATN is a clinical diagnosis triggered by ischemia, nephrotoxins, or rhabdomyolysis.

- **Acute tubular necrosis from ischemia** — Ischemic acute tubular necrosis is the leading cause of intrinsic AKI after sustained hypotension, often during major surgery. ## Urinary incontinence (2 items)

- **Impaired detrusor contractility** — Overflow incontinence in diabetic patients is most often due to neurogenic bladder caused by autonomic neuropathy leading to impaired detrusor contractility. It presents with chronic painless urinary retention, high post-void residual volume, dribbling, and a distended bladder. Diagnosis is confirmed by measuring residual volume, and management begins with bladder decompression followed by cholinergic stimulation using agents like bethanechol.
- **Uninhibited detrusor contractions** — Urge incontinence is caused by involuntary detrusor muscle contractions, leading to sudden urgency and unintentional urine loss. In multiple sclerosis, demyelination disrupts descending inhibitory pathways, resulting in detrusor hyperactivity and frequent, small-volume voids. Management focuses on reducing bladder muscle excitability using antimuscarinic agents like oxybutynin.
